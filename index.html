The Product Journey

I've mentored hundreds of PMs, and the same two problems kept coming up.

  • Problem 1: you can't track specific companies. When a PM is looking for a new job, the biggest challenge is knowing when a new role opens up at a company they're interested in. LinkedIn and the other tools send general updates that may fit your profile, but you can't track the specific companies you care about.
  • Problem 2: there's no good place to practice. Once you're interviewing, mock practice lives in dozens of portals, Slack channels, WhatsApp threads, and Google Sheets of "do you have time to mock?" Practicing case studies and answers is a skill.

That's what I'm solving with NewPMJobs.

  • The solution today: NewPMJobs checks 1,281 companies' job boards every morning and emails 200+ PMs (as of July 2026) the new roles, only for the companies they picked. An AI interview coach for the practice half is in beta.
  • How it's built: Every product decision is mine. I worked with AI to write every line of code, and implemented an AI Tribunal reviewer system where ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude Fable review my code, with Claude Opus as the judge.

Even twenty real users were enough to expose failures that never showed up when I was the only one using it. More detail
5HardeningdoneEarning trust. The longest stretch of the build: self-healing scrapers, alarms on every layer, a login overhaul after sign-ins were quietly failing, and the night the daily job died silently, which taught me why watchdogs exist.Catch your own failures. The system has to notice a failure before a user does, and the alarm has to live outside the thing it watches. More detail
6Betain betaThe interview coach. The second product bet, for the same audience: an AI interview coach. It started as a voice interviewer, gained a lip-synced video avatar and a scored feedback report, and is now in a small invite-only beta where testers pay for their sessions.Riskiest piece first. I tested the hardest part, a voice AI running a real mock interview, before building anything else around it. More detail
7Pre-productionin progressScaling the catalog, then making it browsable. I grew the catalog from 216 to 1,281 companies, built the machine that verifies every new company through the product's own job filter, and taught the product to write its weekly LinkedIn post in my voice. Then a user wrote in with three words about the jobs page showing nothing. The "All Jobs" view had been scoping results to each person's tracked companies since the multi-user overhaul, so a brand new user with zero subscriptions got an empty list while 3,600+ active roles sat in the catalog. Un-scoped it, and added a toggle so folks who want just their tracked list can have it back. The preference saves to the profile and sticks on the next visit.Question your own rules. The catalog was stuck because I assumed every listed company had to be scraped daily. Dropping that assumption removed the ceiling. More detail

Seven Stories From the Build

1
Silent failures

The failures that look like success

I thought my scrapers were running fine. It turns out a scraper that returns zero jobs looks exactly like a company with no openings, and an email marked "sent" doesn't tell you whether it was ever delivered. Bugs like that hide behind healthy-looking numbers, and most of this build was hunting them down. That's why this is story number one.

Read the full story
2
Self-healing scrapers

The scrapers fix themselves now

Broken scrapers used to be my daily chore: an error email, a hand diagnosis, a hand fix. So I taught the system to re-read the careers page, verify the new board really belongs to that company, and repair its own config. Before trusting it, I replayed my six worst real breakages through it: two healed exactly like my hand fixes, one retired a dead board, and three refused to guess and told me why.

3
AI checking AI

The AI Tribunal

The AI that builds the product used to be the only thing reviewing its own work. Now ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude Fable read every change, with Claude Opus as the judge, and the first thing the panel reviewed was itself. A second model found a real security hole the model that wrote the code walked right by.

4
Listening to one user

One email: "Unable to login"

Three words from a stranger. The data behind them: half of iPhone LinkedIn sign-ins failing, corporate mail scanners eating one-time login links before humans could click them. Two fixes, and a daily alarm so the front door can't break again without me knowing.

5
Monitoring the monitors

The daily job died and nothing told me

Right after I doubled the catalog, a deploy killed the daily run partway through. No email went out and nothing alerted me, because the alarm died with the server it ran on. The watchdog now lives outside the app, and a monthly probe tests the alarms themselves.

6
The next bet, in beta now

An AI interview coach

Once people started finding roles through the product, the next question was always the same: now I need to practice. I'd watched PMs organize mocks through Slack channels and Google Sheets for years, so I built the practice half: a voice interviewer that actually reads your prep, a lip-synced face, a scored feedback report, and a small invite-only beta where testers pay for their sessions.

Read the full story
7
Listening to the catalog

3,600 roles, one filter, zero showing

A user wrote in: no jobs on the jobs page. Zero subscriptions, 3,600+ active roles in the catalog, and a filter I'd added during the multi-user build that scoped "All Jobs" to only the companies you track. I'd never caught it because I always had subscriptions myself. Un-scoping it was the right call. Jobs are public data, and the logged-out feed was already showing every row to anonymous visitors, so there was no privacy argument for hiding them from someone who just signed up. I added a toggle so folks who do want just their tracked list can flip back, and it saves to the profile and sticks.
